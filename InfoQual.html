Information Quality

Definition of Information Quality

Information quality refers to the degree to which information is accurate, reliable, relevant, timely and complete. Assessment of the quality of information is crucial in making informed decisions and accurate conclusions.

Criteria for Information Quality

Accuracy: The information is free from mistakes and errors, and accurately reflects the meaning of data on which its based (Thakur, 2023).


Relevance: Information should be suitable to the topic at hand (Thakur, 2023).


Reliability: When information is accurate and reliable over time and is not subject to fluctuations or changes (Thakur, 2023).


Timeliness: Is when information is up-to-date and is available for its intended use. (Mallach, 2020)


Completeness: It ensures that the information gathered gives the complete picture of reality and not a part of it. (Thakur, 2023)


Precision: It implies the accuracy of information in relation to the query set by the user. (Mallach, 2020)


Consistency: When the data remains the same and does not contradict itself when accessed or modified by users (Mallach, 2020).


Conformity to needs and expectations: When Information conforms to the needs and expectations of those who will use it, such as users or business enterprises.


Cost: The cost of gathering information using resources to gain an upper hand in the market.


Criteria for Information Quality

Goal-based evaluation: This method ensures that a particular information system meets its intended goals. One example would be an evaluation of a shopping website measuring how well it facilitates purchases (Bhat, 2023).


Formative evaluation: This method evaluates an information system during its development without clear goals to identify and correct problems. An example of this would be testing a new software application with users to identify issues in usability (Chen, et al., 2011).


Summative evaluation: The method evaluates the effectiveness of an information system after being implemented. For example, a summative evaluation of a hospital's electronic health record system would measure its impact on patient outcomes (Chen, et al., 2011).


Criteria-based evaluation: This method involves evaluating an information system based on predefined checklists, heuristics, or criteria. An example would be an evaluation of a trading platform, measuring its compliance with financial and privacy regulations (Chen, et al., 2011).


Goal-free evaluation: This method involves the evaluation of an information system without preconceived notions of what it should accomplish. For example, a goal-free evaluation of a search engine would measure how well it retrieves relevant results (Chen, et al., 2011).

Data Ethics

What is Data Ethics

Referred to as a branch of ethics that evaluate data practices, such as the collection, generation, analyzation, and dissemination of data that is either structured or unstructured, ultimately having the potential to impact people, businesses, and society.

Importance of data ethics in the modern age:

Since people increasingly live their lives online, the line between private and public has been blurred. Companies, in particular technology companies sell personal information to third parties, that will ultimately make these companies huge profit, but risking the privacy and safety of their clueless customers.

According to Harvard, only 23% of people who search the web know that they are sharing their personal information.

Data ethics combat that by enforcing rules and regulations such as the EU's General Data Protection Regulation and California's CCPA that ensures that consumers have more control over the personal information that businesses collect about them, and that business adheres to ethical ways of retrieving information.

Case Studies of companies not using data ethics efficiently:

Ethical algorithms error

Amazon's Hiring Algorithms:

Amazon created a hiring algorithm that would decide which resumes made it to the next hiring stage to combat issues involving finding candidates for employment. However, a major issue occurs when the system hired more men than women due to bias that was created due to historical data involved with employment, which resulted in the algorithm singling out women without prejudice.


Facial Recognition struggles to detect darker skin tones:

Facial recognition is widely used, from accessing phones and accounts, to being used as a critical tool for law enforcement, such as surveillance and screening in airports. However, in 2018, research revealed these algorithms performed worse on dark-skinned females, up to 34% error rates than that of light-skinned males.

Uber Tracking individuals:

In 2014, reports claimed that Uber's “god-mode” gave all employees access to complete user data. It was used to track celebrities, politicians and personal connections (such as ex-partners).


Uber cybersecurity attack::

In October 2016, Uber tried to cover up a massive cybersecurity attack that exposed confidential data of 57 million customers and drivers. In 2022, they were found guilty of not reporting this to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC).

Principles of Big Data Ethics

The essential aim of these principals is to translate basic human rights into the digital age. The motivation behind these principals is:


Harms to people: Impacts person's civil liberties or physical safety, discrimination of a certain group or repression of democratic participation.


Harms to organization: Impacts on business operations, such as monetary loss, security breaches and harm of reputation.


Harms to systems: On the large scale, such as the financial system.


Some of these principles include:


Transparency: It is clear communication of what data will be gathered, where the data will be stored, and who to share the data with. This is usually done through Terms and Conditions (T&Cs), informing customers how the company will use their information.


Accountability: The organization should take responsibility for what happens to an individual's data after collecting it, whether it be data leaks or information being stolen by hackers, companies should ensure that they take accountability for damages their processes can cause users.


Individual Agency: Agency refers to a person's ability to make choices about their personal data. Whether it involves removing their own data from a website or allowing a company to use their details, allowing individuals to control what happens to their information should be a human right.


Data Privacy: Privacy refers to one's ability to expect protection from public exposure of their data. As a business, you should protect the user's data if they allow for their personal information to be used.


The business benefits of Data Ethics

Trust: Businesses that apply key ethical principles of privacy, transparency and accountability to their systems will build greater goodwill and loyalty, thus enhancing their reputation and brand value.


Fair practices: Unintended bias can negatively impact business decisions. Companies adhering to fair ethics practices and standards can positively impact business decisions and ensure fairness in decision making.


Data privacy compliance: Data privacy compliance such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) overlaps between privacy requirements such as lawfulness, accountability, and the principles of AI ethics. It ensures ethical AI helps ensure data privacy compliance.


Data Ethics Guidance for Organizational Leaders

Startups: Startups should include technology and tools including AI applications as well as mindfulness about ethical implications. It's important that these companies develop these technological processes early on.


Small/Medium-Sized Businesses: As small and medium businesses begin to scale, they should add more technology that gathers user data, develop databases, and hire engineers to manage these systems. Leaders should also prioritize transparency and accessibility as they grow. They should also keep customers informed about the data they have gathered as well as how it will be used to develop its processes.


Global Corporations: Due to the scale of these corporations, developing reliable security, privacy and ethics processes are even more critical to prevent ransomware, leaks and immoral use of data by employees. They should also develop internal, cross-departmental working groups. Enterprises should also expand training for their employees and incentivize regular analysis for managers and employees.


Regulations

Businesses should ensure that they adhere to all laws and regulations regarding data ethics:


Data Protection:

  • CCPR, CCPA (USA-California)
  • GDPR (EU)


AI Use:

  • EU AI Act
  • Algorithm Justice and Online Platform Transparency Act (USA)
  • New York Bias in Hiring AI Regulation (USA-NY))


Platform Regulation:

  • Digital Services Act (European Union)
